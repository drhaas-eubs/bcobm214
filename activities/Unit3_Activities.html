Customer Discovery — Role Play Session

Signature pedagogy: Role Play. Today, students stop talking about customers and start talking as them — and learn how badly designed most discovery interviews are.

The Six Roles

RoleBrief
FounderHas just walked into a meeting with a potential customer. Goal: learn whether the customer has the problem the founder thinks they have. Trap: avoid pitching.
The Polite CustomerLikes the founder, doesn't want to disappoint, will agree with everything. Brief: speak in compliments, never specifics.
The Specific CustomerHas the problem, has tried three solutions, can name what they paid and when. Brief: only answer when asked specific questions about specific past behaviours.
The Wrong CustomerLooks like the target on paper but actually has a different job-to-be-done. Brief: keep redirecting the conversation to your problem.
Observer AWatches with the Mom Test rubric. Records every leading question.
Observer BWatches with the JTBD lens. Records the actual job the customer mentions.

The Three Scenes

  1. Scene 1 — The Polite Conversation (15 min)Founder interviews the Polite Customer. Observers note. Debrief: how many leading questions did the founder ask? How many compliments masked actual lack of intent?
  2. Scene 2 — The Specific Conversation (15 min)Same founder, different customer (the Specific one). Observers note again. Debrief: where did the conversation get rich? Almost always when the founder asked about past behaviour rather than future intent.
  3. Scene 3 — The Wrong Customer (15 min)Same founder, the Wrong Customer. Critical learning: the founder must notice this is the wrong customer and pivot the conversation, not push the script. Debrief: when did the founder realise? What was the signal?

Group Configuration

For 12 students: form 2 groups of 6. Each group rotates through the three scenes, with roles re-cast each scene so every student plays at least two distinct roles in 70 minutes.

The Mom Test Rubric (Observer A)

Score every question

· Past behaviour ("When did you last…?") — +1
· Specific facts ("How much did you pay?") — +1
· Hypothetical ("Would you use…?") — −1
· Pitching ("Imagine if we built…") — −1
· Compliment fishing ("Do you think this is a good idea?") — −2

Synthesise — TAM-SAM-SOM (15 min)

From the role play, each student now has at least one signal of who their customer actually is. They sketch a TAM-SAM-SOM for the surviving idea, sized using the role-play insights and 5 minutes of desk research. The point is not precision — it is to confirm whether the SOM is a market or a meeting.

Reflect (10 min)

  • What did your customer say that you didn't expect?
  • What did you almost say that would have been a Mom Test violation?
  • If you had to redesign your product after today, what would change?

Close — Bring to Unit 4

Pre-work for Unit 4 (Hackathon)

· Bring a filled-in Lean Canvas (best draft) for your surviving idea
· Bring three direct quotes from real customers (post role play, post conversations)
· Bring your SOM number with one sentence of reasoning
