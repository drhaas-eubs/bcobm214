Growth Boardroom — Role Play Session

Signature pedagogy: Role Play. Today, students take seats at the board of a fast-growing founder-led venture facing the Greiner curve's first major crisis — and must decide whether the founder should remain CEO.

The Five Roles

RoleBrief
Founder-CEOBuilt the company from zero to €18m revenue in 5 years. Believes the firm is on track. Privately: exhausted, working 80-hour weeks, struggling to delegate. Briefed with personal financial position (significant equity but no liquidity).
Series A Lead InvestorHolds 22% post-Series A. Has seen the same pattern at three prior portfolio companies. Privately convinced the firm needs a professional CEO; politely doesn't want to fire the founder.
Independent Board DirectorFormer operator at a similar-stage firm. Thinks the founder can grow into the role with the right support; the alternative is risky.
Co-founder / CTOHas been quietly disagreeing with the CEO for 18 months. Admires their vision; frustrated by their inability to delegate. Doesn't want the CEO seat themselves.
Head of Growth (board observer, no vote)Joined 9 months ago from a scale-up. Privately considering leaving if the founder doesn't change. Briefed with employee survey data: trust in CEO declining.

Phase-by-Phase Facilitation

  1. Open · 10 minHook: Show the class a short video clip of a founder-CEO transition story (e.g., Travis Kalanick at Uber, or Adam Neumann at WeWork). Ask: "What do these have in common?" Land: every fast-growing venture eventually faces the question — and the question is rarely about competence; it's about fit between phase and leader.
  2. Frame · 15 minFaculty draws Greiner's growth curve (F49) on the board. Identifies today's case as sitting on the Leadership Crisis (between Phase 1 Creativity and Phase 2 Direction). Introduces Adizes (F50) and Churchill & Lewis (F51) as alternative diagnostic lenses. Today: a boardroom decides whether the founder should stay, evolve, or step aside.
  3. Explore · 10 minStudents read the Olive Lab case individually. Pre-form their own hypothesis: stay, evolve or step aside.
  4. Apply · 75 min — The BoardroomFor 12 students: form 2-3 boards of 4-5. Each board has the five roles (Head of Growth observes only).

    Round 1 (10 min): Each role reads their private brief. Independent Director chairs.
    Round 2 (15 min): Founder-CEO presents their growth plan and asks for Series B authorisation. Q&A.
    Round 3 (15 min): Investor and Co-founder raise their concerns. Founder responds. Tension allowed.
    Round 4 (20 min): Boardroom debate. Three options on the table: founder stays, founder + chief operating partner brought in, professional CEO recruited. Decide.
    Round 5 (15 min): Once decided, the board must communicate the decision to the founder (if they're still in the role) or to the company (if not). Each board drafts the announcement.
  5. Synthesise · 15 minPlenary. Each board reports their verdict and the announcement they drafted. Faculty captures the patterns: which boards trusted the founder to grow? Which didn't? What argument tipped each call?
  6. Reflect · 10 minSolo writing:
    · Whose perspective in the boardroom did you find most uncomfortable to take seriously?
    · If you were the founder, what would you most need from the board to actually grow into the role?
    · What sign would convince you, as a board member, that the founder had transitioned successfully?
  7. Close · 15 minFaculty preview Unit 8 (Funding Constraint Cards Hackathon). Pre-reading: The capital stack decoded. Bring last week's plan financials for funding scenario work.
