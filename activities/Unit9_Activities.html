Pitch Craft Studio — Presentation Session Guide

Signature pedagogy: Presentation. Today, every student delivers their pitch three times in the same session — each time iterating after structured feedback — and learns that the third version is always materially better than the first.

Phase-by-Phase Facilitation

  1. Open · 10 minHook: Show the same one-minute Apple product launch reel from 2007 (Steve Jobs introducing the iPhone). Then show a forgettable but technically competent product launch from a different company. Ask: "What's the gap?" Land: pitch craft is not optional skill; it is the channel through which strategic substance becomes investor commitment.
  2. Frame · 15 minFaculty walks through the three architectural frameworks: Kawasaki's 10/20/30 rule (F65), Sequoia's pitch deck template (F66), and Heath Brothers' SUCCESs (F67). Then introduces three delivery frameworks: Mehrabian (F69), Aristotle's ethos-pathos-logos (F70), and Cuddy's power posing (F72). Today's discipline: pitch three times, iterate after each.
  3. Explore · 10 minStudents individually browse the Unit 9 framework gallery. Identify the architectural framework they will use as backbone for their pitch.
  4. Apply · 75 min — The StudioFor 12 students: form 4 triads. Each student delivers their venture pitch three times to the same triad, with restructured feedback after each.

    Round 1 (25 min): Each student pitches for 90 seconds. Triad members give two pieces of feedback only — one on architecture (was the structure clear?), one on delivery (did the speaker hold attention?). 5 min per pitch + 3 min feedback = 8 min × 3 = 24 min.

    Round 2 (25 min): Same pitches, refined. Triad members now use Aristotle's three appeals (F70) as the audit lens: was ethos established? was pathos engaged? was logos coherent? 8 min per pitch.

    Round 3 (25 min): Final pitch. This time: full 90 seconds with no notes, standing, projected timer, after a 2-minute power pose preparation (F72). Triad members score 1-5 on six SUCCESs attributes. Each student receives a written scorecard.
  5. Synthesise · 15 minPlenary. The triads each nominate one final-round pitch they found most improved between Round 1 and Round 3. Three nominees deliver their final pitch to the full class. Class votes on the most improved (not the best — the most improved). Faculty captures patterns: what did the iteration cycle do that one-shot pitching cannot?
  6. Reflect · 10 minSolo writing:
    · What did you change between Round 1 and Round 3?
    · Which feedback was hardest to accept?
    · If you had to deliver this pitch tomorrow to a real investor, what is one thing you would change again?
  7. Close · 15 minFaculty preview Unit 10 (Capstone Business Challenge). Pre-reading: What investors read in 90 seconds. Bring final pitch + final business plan for capstone.

Differentiation Notes

For students with public-speaking anxiety

Allow them to deliver Round 1 seated. Move to standing only by Round 2. The repetition itself reduces anxiety more reliably than any technique.

For confident pitchers

Add a constraint at Round 3: deliver the pitch in 60 seconds (one-third less time). Compression usually exposes which content was load-bearing and which was decorative.
