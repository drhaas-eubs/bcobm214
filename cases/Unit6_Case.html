Helvetia Industries — The Innovator Inside

A teaching case for Unit 6 — used to test whether a corporate boardroom can recognise an H3 opportunity when it stands in the room and asks for funding.

The Company

Helvetia Industries is a 91-year-old Swiss precision-engineering company headquartered in Winterthur. It manufactures industrial gear assemblies, predominantly for energy infrastructure (hydroelectric and wind turbine drivetrains). Annual revenue: CHF 320 million, with operating margins of 14% — historically strong, currently under pressure from low-cost Asian competitors. The firm employs 920 people, is third-generation family-owned, and has paid dividends for 38 consecutive years.

The Three Initiatives Up for Funding

The annual portfolio review board meets next Tuesday. Three initiatives compete for the CHF 18 million innovation budget.

Initiative A — Manufacturing Excellence Programme (H1)

A continuous-improvement programme to reduce defect rates in the existing gear-assembly line by 35% over 18 months. CHF 12 million capex. Expected payback: 2.5 years through reduced warranty claims and rework. Sponsored by the COO. Vetted, low-risk, predictable.

Initiative B — Wind Sector Service Pivot (H2)

Build out a predictive-maintenance services business for wind-turbine operators, leveraging Helvetia's deep knowledge of drivetrain failure modes. CHF 10 million over 3 years to build the service team and software platform. Estimated CHF 28 million annual recurring revenue by year 5. Sponsored by the Head of Wind. Higher uncertainty but clearly adjacent.

Initiative C — Industrial Robotics Spin-Out (H3)

Spin out a small (12-person) team developing collaborative-robotics arms for small-batch manufacturing — a non-Helvetia category, but technically related to the firm's precision-mechanics expertise. Founded by Dr. Andreas Fehr (38, ex-ETH Zürich, joined Helvetia 4 years ago after his own startup was acquired). Andreas wants CHF 8 million over 24 months to build a pilot product and ten reference customers. Most board members have never met Andreas. He has been working on the side with two engineers and a part-time designer for 18 months.

Total available budget: CHF 18 million. The board can fully fund any one initiative, partially fund any two, or all three at reduced scope.

The Politics

The CFO is privately briefed by the family chairman (whose dividend depends on next-year EBIT) to favour Initiative A. The CIO has championed Initiative C internally for six months. The COO is sceptical of anything that distracts from manufacturing. The CEO is new (six months in role) and wants to signal both fiscal discipline and innovation ambition — but cannot do both with this budget.

The Question

The board must decide:

  1. Which initiative(s) to fund, and at what scale
  2. What conditions to attach
  3. What governance to put around any H3 commitment
  4. What signal the decision sends to the rest of the organisation

The teaching tension

Initiative A is the rationally-defensible choice in any single quarter. Initiative C is the choice that, in 10 years, separates the Helvetias that survive from the Helvetias that quietly decline. The board has to live with both timeframes simultaneously.

Source: Teaching case developed for BCOBM214 by Dr. Hildegard Haas. Helvetia Industries is fictional; situational dynamics inspired by Swiss industrial sector reports (KOF, 2024-25), three-horizon allocation literature (Baghai et al. 1999), and corporate-venturing case research at Tuck and HBS.
