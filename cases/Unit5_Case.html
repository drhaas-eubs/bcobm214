BCOBM214 · Unit 5 · Case Study

Aria Wines — A Business Plan that Almost Worked

A teaching case for Unit 5 — used to identify which of Eisenmann's six patterns of startup failure killed an otherwise plausible venture, and which the original plan could have addressed.

The Founders

Sofia Marchetti (32, ex-management consultant) and Matteo Conti (35, third-generation Tuscan winemaker) met in 2022 at a wine-tech conference in Verona. They co-founded Aria Wines in early 2023 with a clear thesis: small Italian wine producers were producing extraordinary wines, but selling them through fragmented distribution at painfully low margins. Aria would aggregate 30-50 small producers, sell direct-to-consumer in Northern Europe via subscription, and capture the margin currently going to importers and retailers.

The Original Plan (Q1 2023)

SectionPlan
CustomerAffluent Northern European wine drinkers, 35-55, willing to pay €25-€40 per bottle
Value PropositionCurated boxes of small-producer Italian wine delivered monthly, with stories of the producers
ChannelsDirect-to-consumer subscription via custom website
Revenue Model€89/month for 3 bottles + tasting notes
Funding€450,000 seed round (raised in Q2 2023)
Year-1 Target3,500 active subscribers

What Actually Happened

Q3 2023: Site launched. 200 subscribers in first quarter (vs target of 700). Producer relationships strong; logistics OK; conversion rate from website visit to subscription was 0.4% versus the 2.1% the plan assumed.

Q1 2024: Pivoted to add an "à la carte" single-bottle store alongside subscription. Spike in revenue, but mostly from existing subscribers buying additional bottles, not new customers.

Q3 2024: Pivoted again to a B2B channel — selling to small wine bars in Amsterdam, Berlin and Stockholm. Promising early signal but fundamentally a different business with different unit economics.

Q1 2025: Cash ran out. The team had executed every pivot competently. The wines were excellent. The producer relationships were enviable. 870 active subscribers (24% of target) — the unit economics worked at scale, but they never reached scale.

Q2 2025: Aria closed. Sofia returned to consulting. Matteo returned to the family vineyard.

The Post-Mortem

Sofia later wrote:

"We had a real opportunity. We had a real product. We had real customers — just not enough of them, fast enough. The plan we wrote in 2023 was internally coherent but it required several things to be true that we never tested: that direct-to-consumer wine subscription would convert at industry-typical rates in our segment, that customer-acquisition cost would stay under €60, and that we'd find a marketing channel cheap enough to scale. None of those things turned out to be true. We pivoted twice — both pivots were intelligent, but each consumed three months of cash without producing a defensible new model."

Your Task

You are the post-mortem analyst. Using Eisenmann's Six Patterns of Startup Failure (F37):

  1. Score Aria 1-5 on vulnerability to each of the six patterns at the original plan stage (Q1 2023)
  2. Identify the two patterns that most contributed to the eventual failure
  3. Propose: what would the original 2023 plan have looked like if those two patterns had been audited before the seed raise?
  4. Write Sofia and Matteo a 4-sentence message: what should the founder of an analogous venture do differently from day one?

The teaching point

Aria didn't fail from incompetence or bad luck. It failed from a plan whose foundational assumptions were never falsifiable in the time and capital available. That pattern is the most common startup death — and the most preventable.
