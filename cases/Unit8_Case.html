BCOBM214 · Unit 8 · Case Study

Solero Power — Bootstrap, Angel or VC?

A teaching case for Unit 8 — used to test whether students can match a venture's actual situation to the right capital partner, rather than reflexively reaching for the most prestigious option.

The Company

Solero Power is a Madrid-based clean-energy startup founded in 2024 by Pablo Ruiz (29, ex-Iberdrola engineer) and Carmen Vega (31, ex-management consultant at Boston Consulting Group). Their product is a small-scale solar-plus-battery system optimised for Spanish residential use under the country's recent self-consumption regulation. The system pays back in 5-7 years and includes a software layer that participates in grid-balancing markets, sharing revenue with homeowners.

Status (Q4 2025)

  • 72 systems installed across Madrid and Toledo
  • Average revenue per system: €4,200 installation + €18/month software fee
  • Gross margin per installation: 28%
  • Founders working full-time, 4 part-time installers contracted
  • Cash runway: 4 months at current burn
  • Pipeline: 340 qualified leads waiting for installation, capacity-constrained

The Three Term Sheets on the Table

Option A — Family & Angel Round (FFF + Angels, F58)

Pablo's family + a small group of Madrid-based angel investors (one is Pablo's former Iberdrola manager) have offered €350,000 at a €2.4M pre-money valuation. Documentation: simple SAFEs (F64). Conditions: no board seats, quarterly written updates. Money available within 30 days.

Option B — Specialist Climate Tech VC (Series Seed, F60)

Verde Ventures, a small Iberian climate-tech fund, offers €1.8M at a €6.5M pre-money on a priced equity round. Conditions: one board seat, monthly board meetings, an option pool of 12% to be created in pre-money (founder-diluting). Money available in 4-5 months after diligence.

Option C — Bootstrapping (F57) + Customer Pre-Payments

Solero could offer a 10% installation discount in exchange for full pre-payment from the 340 qualified leads. Conservative estimate: 60-80 customers would accept (~€250k cash within 60 days). No dilution; full control retained. But: capacity constraint forces Solero to delay 80%+ of the qualified pipeline by 6+ months — letting competitors potentially capture them.

The Founders' Disagreement

Pablo wants Option A. He is uncomfortable taking VC money, wants to retain control, and believes Solero is fundamentally a profitable installation business not a hyper-growth tech company. He thinks Verde Ventures will push for unrealistic growth.

Carmen wants Option B. She believes the regulatory window in Spain is open for ~36 months and that capital is the only way to capture the pipeline before competitors arrive. She thinks Verde's network and reputation will accelerate enterprise channel deals.

Neither has seriously considered Option C; they regard it as "not really fundraising."

Your Task

You are an outside advisor brought in by the founders to break their deadlock. Using three frameworks from Unit 8:

  1. Apply the Angel·VC·PE Spectrum (F60) — which capital partner actually fits Solero's profile?
  2. Build the cap table (F62) for the chosen option, projected 24 months out
  3. Apply the Funding Curve (F61) — what does the next round look like, and how does today's choice constrain it?
  4. Write Pablo and Carmen a 4-sentence message: which option do you recommend and why?

The teaching point

The most common founder mistake is to default to the option that signals success (VC term sheet) rather than the option that fits the venture's actual economics. Solero's 28% installation margin and capacity-constrained pipeline are not VC-curve economics. The right answer here is rarely the most prestigious one.
