SilentRoom — A Co-Working Idea Looking for a Customer

A teaching case for Unit 3. The idea sounds plausible. The market data looks supportive. The customer interviews are quietly screaming.

The Concept

Tomás and Anja are EU Business School Year 3 students from Munich. In late 2025 they began designing a concept they call SilentRoom: a chain of small (40m²) "deep-work cells" embedded in residential neighbourhoods of European cities — Berlin, Vienna, Lisbon, Barcelona — where remote-working professionals could rent a soundproofed, beautifully designed solo workspace by the hour, on demand, via app.

Their thesis: WeWork-style co-working maximised serendipity. SilentRoom would maximise focus. Their target was the under-served "deep work" market: knowledge workers who needed concentration more than collaboration.

The Market Data Looked Good

  • European remote-working workforce in 2025: ~38 million.
  • 72% reported "frequent or constant" struggles with focus at home (McKinsey survey, 2024).
  • Co-working membership growth in Europe: 11.4% YoY for 2023–24 (Statista).
  • Average price for solo desk in central Berlin co-working space: €280/month.

The Discovery Interviews

Tomás and Anja conducted 22 customer discovery interviews over six weeks. They came back delighted: 19 of the 22 said "I would absolutely use this." Three said "I'd pay €25/hour, easily."

They returned to faculty for review. Their advisor, Dr. Haas, asked one question: "Of those 22, how many had ever paid for a co-working day pass before?"

The answer: none.

"Of those 22," she continued, "how many had ever rented a hotel room or library carrel just to work?"

The answer: two — and both for less than €30 total in the past 12 months.

The Quiet Signal

Tomás and Anja had asked: "Would you use SilentRoom?" They had not asked: "Have you ever paid to be more focused?"

Past behaviour, not future intent.

What Happened Next

They went back to four of the most enthusiastic interviewees and asked, instead, three Mom Test questions:

  1. "In the last month, what's the most expensive thing you bought to help your work?"
  2. "In the last month, where did you actually go when you couldn't focus at home?"
  3. "What did you spend on it?"

The four answers, by interviewee:

None of these four had paid €25 in their entire career to access focus space. They had paid hundreds of euros to own tools that improved focus. Different job-to-be-done.

Your Task

  1. Diagnose the original 22-interview signal using F20 The Mom Test.
  2. Apply F12 JTBD: what job is this customer hiring tools for? Is it the same job SilentRoom solves?
  3. Apply F22 Four Actions Grid: should SilentRoom Eliminate · Reduce · Raise · Create anything to find a real customer? Or kill the idea?
  4. Write Tomás and Anja a 4-sentence message: should they kill, pivot, or persevere — and why?

The teaching point

This case is not about SilentRoom being a bad idea. It's about discovery interviews that flatter founders rather than informing them. A correctly-designed interview from week one would have changed the venture — or ended it humanely — months earlier.
