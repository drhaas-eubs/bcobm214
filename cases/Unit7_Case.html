Olive Lab — From Founder-led to Professionally Run

A teaching case for Unit 7 — used to test whether a board can navigate the Greiner Leadership Crisis without breaking the company that reached the crisis through the founder's brilliance.

The Company

Olive Lab is a Marseille-based natural skincare brand founded in late 2020 by Léa Marot (now 34), a former cosmetic chemist at L'Oréal. The brand sells a tightly curated range of 12 plant-based products through its own website (60% of revenue), French pharmacy chain Citypharma (25%), and Sephora France (15%). Revenue trajectory: €0.4m (2021), €1.8m (2022), €5.6m (2023), €11.2m (2024), €17.9m (2025). Headcount: 41 people. Series A of €4.2m closed in early 2024; runway through Q3 2026 if growth continues at current pace.

Léa

Léa is the company's product genius. Every formulation — including the now-iconic Olive Restorative Balm that launched the brand — was created by her. Customers love her: she answers Instagram DMs personally, hosts monthly "lab days" where loyal customers test prototypes, and speaks fluently to retailers about formulation chemistry.

She is also the bottleneck. The leadership team (CFO, CMO, COO — all hired in 2024) report a familiar pattern: decisions queue at her desk, she works late nights to clear them, and she resists hiring people who would reduce her direct involvement in product development. Her co-founder and CTO, Hugo Rey (37, ex-Yves Rocher), has been managing operations as a parallel track but increasingly avoids strategic conversations because he and Léa "talk past each other."

The Strategic Inflection

The Series A was raised on a plan that anticipated geographic expansion (Italy, Germany, the UK) starting in mid-2026. The lead investor, Aurora Capital, holds 22%. Aurora's partner Marc Lefèvre has now been on the board for 18 months. He has watched four prior portfolio companies face this exact transition and has, privately, formed a strong view.

The board is meeting next Friday to authorise a Series B fundraising target (the firm needs ~€15m to fund the European expansion plan). Marc has indicated to the chair that he wants to discuss leadership before authorising the round.

The Three Options

The board has three plausible paths:

  1. Founder-CEO continues, with stronger COO authority. Léa stays as CEO; existing COO given expanded mandate; Léa coached on delegation. Lowest disruption. Requires Léa to genuinely change.
  2. Léa transitions to Chief Product Officer; new CEO hired. Léa retains product authority; Aurora helps recruit a professional CEO with European expansion experience. Highest predictability of execution. Requires Léa to accept the change.
  3. Léa stays as CEO + Chief Operating Partner brought in alongside. A senior operator (likely with prior CEO experience) joins as #2 with broad operational authority. Léa keeps the title but shares the role. Compromise position. May be the worst of all worlds — or the best.

The Board's Question

The board must decide which option to pursue, on what timeline, and how to communicate the decision — to Léa first, then to the team, then to the broader market — without breaking the company that exists today.

The teaching tension

The data suggests the founder needs to evolve or step aside. The brand is the founder. The transition that protects the business may break the brand; the transition that protects the brand may break the business.
