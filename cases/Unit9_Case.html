BCOBM214 · Unit 9 · Case Study

Foglio — Eight Pitches in One Week

A teaching case for Unit 9 — used to test what changes between pitch one and pitch eight, and why iteration under live pressure is the only reliable training for high-stakes pitching.

The Founder

Elena Bianchi (28) is the founder of Foglio, a Milan-based startup making professional-grade paper notebooks designed to be paired with handwriting-recognition apps. The product turns physical handwriting into searchable, editable digital text without requiring a specialised tablet or pen — using the camera on any modern smartphone. Foglio's first product launched in early 2025 and sold 14,000 units in its first six months, mostly through a viral TikTok campaign.

The Week

In late October 2025, Foglio entered a fundraising sprint. Elena flew to London on Monday morning and pitched eight investors over four days: three angels, three early-stage VCs, one corporate venture arm of a Japanese stationery firm, and one family office. She used essentially the same deck for every pitch. She delivered the pitch herself, alone, with no co-founder support.

What She Learned (in her own words, from her end-of-week journal)

Pitch 1 — Monday 10am · Angel

"He listened. I think I was nervous, talked too fast. I forgot to mention the cost of acquisition. He didn't ask any hard questions, which I now realise was a bad sign. He said 'this is interesting, send me the deck'. I sent it. He didn't reply."

Pitch 2 — Monday 2pm · Climate-tech VC

"She told me 15 minutes in that they don't invest in consumer products. I should have looked her up properly. Worst pitch of the week — I tried to pivot the narrative mid-pitch and lost coherence."

Pitch 3 — Tuesday 9am · Family office

"They asked about unit economics first. I had the numbers but not in front of me. Lost their attention. Should never have buried the unit economics on slide 11."

Pitch 4 — Tuesday 1pm · Angel (referred by Pitch 1)

"I had restructured the deck overnight. Started with the moment a customer films their notebook page and watches the text appear in their phone — pure 'aha'. He leaned forward. He said 'I'm in for €30k' before I'd finished. First positive signal of the week."

Pitch 5 — Wednesday 10am · Consumer-tech VC

"Big firm, three partners in the room. I led with the moment-of-aha again. They liked the product but pushed hard on defensibility — what stops Moleskine or Apple from copying us in 12 months? I had a defensible answer (the OCR pipeline takes 18 months to build, and we're 14 months in) but I delivered it without conviction. They thanked me. No follow-up so far."

Pitch 6 — Wednesday 3pm · Japanese corporate venture

"They were thoughtful. Asked about manufacturing. I knew this answer cold. Asked about Asia. I improvised. They said they'd circle back. I think they're a real prospect — but only because I matched their slow pace."

Pitch 7 — Thursday 10am · Angel

"By now I had cut the deck from 14 slides to 9. Started with product demo (live, on my phone). Talked about the team for one minute. Got to traction quickly. He asked sharp questions. He wrote a €50k cheque on the spot. The third positive signal of the week."

Pitch 8 — Thursday 4pm · Early-stage VC

"Last pitch. Tired. Delivered the leanest version yet — 7 minutes including questions. The partner said 'you've clearly been pitching all week — you're sharp now'. They committed to a follow-up call. I think there's a deal there."

Your Task

You are Elena's pitch coach, debriefing her on Sunday. Using three frameworks from Unit 9:

  1. Diagnose what changed between pitches 1-3 and pitches 7-8 using F65, F66 and F67
  2. Identify the single change that produced the largest improvement (and what framework it maps to)
  3. Apply F69 (Mehrabian) and F72 (power posing) — what do you suspect changed in delivery, not just content, between Monday and Thursday?
  4. Write Elena three sentences: what to do differently in next month's pitch sprint

The teaching point

Pitch craft is not learnt in classrooms. It is learnt by pitching, getting feedback, and pitching again. The 8-pitch arc is the cheapest training money cannot buy — and most founders skip it because they treat each pitch as terminal rather than iterative.

Source: Teaching case developed for BCOBM214 by Dr. Hildegard Haas. Foglio is fictional; the iteration arc is consistent with patterns reported in Y Combinator and Techstars founder retrospectives.
