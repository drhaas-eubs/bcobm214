BCOBM214 · Unit 6 · Reflection

Who Lights the Third Horizon?

Three independent-study prompts. ~30 minutes. Bring written responses to Session 7.

Question 1 — The role you held

In Session 6 you played one of four roles (CFO, CIO, Intrapreneur, CEO). Write 6-8 sentences:

  • What did you understand about the Three-Horizon problem from inside that role that you didn't understand from outside it?
  • Which of the other three roles did you find hardest to take seriously? Why?
  • Which Pinchot commandment did your role violate or honour most clearly?

Why this matters

Role plays produce empathy through inhabitation. Most criticism of corporate decision-making comes from people who have never sat in the seat. The reflection is in the difference between what looked obvious from outside and what felt necessary from inside.

Question 2 — The verdict you made

Your board reached a verdict on Helvetia's three initiatives. In 4-6 sentences:

  • State the verdict
  • State the strongest argument against the verdict
  • If you had 24 more hours, what would you investigate before locking the decision?

Question 3 — Who lights the third horizon

In any firm, there are usually a small number of people who could credibly lead an H3 initiative — but only if the senior team recognises them and gives them the air cover to operate. In 8-10 sentences, write about a firm you know well (current or past employer, family business, internship):

  • Who in that firm could lead an H3 initiative? (Name the role, not the person.)
  • What is currently preventing them from doing so?
  • What would have to change in the firm's culture, governance or capital allocation for them to actually lead?
  • If you were the CEO of that firm, what would your first move be?

How this connects forward

Unit 7 (Growth Management) takes the corporate-context perspective and reverses it: how does a founder-led venture transition into a professionally-managed one? The Greiner curve (F49) and Adizes lifecycle (F50) name the predictable crises of that transition. The boardroom seat you sat in today returns next week — but with a different problem.
