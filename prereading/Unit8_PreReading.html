The Capital Stack Decoded

Bootstrapping, FFF, crowdfunding, angels, VC, PE, debt — different capital sources serve different ventures, and choosing wrongly is one of the most expensive mistakes in entrepreneurial life. Read before Session 8. ~15 minutes.

Why this matters

Most introductory entrepreneurship courses teach fundraising as a single skill: how to pitch a VC. That framing is misleading. Most successful businesses never raise venture capital, and many that do raise it would have built better businesses without it. The discipline this unit teaches is to match capital to venture — picking the source whose incentives, time horizon and value-add align with what the venture actually needs.

Three principles for choosing

Match the capital to the venture, not the venture to the capital

If your business has 25% gross margins and grows linearly, VC will push you toward economics you can't deliver. If your business needs €30M in capex to validate, bootstrapping won't get you there. The capital partner that asks the right questions about your specific economics is more valuable than the capital partner with the most prestigious brand.

Time horizon matters more than capital amount

VC funds typically need to return capital in 7-10 years. PE in 5-7. Angels often have 10+ year horizons. Bootstrap is unbounded. Picking a partner whose time horizon matches yours prevents the slow-motion divorce that kills many ventures.

The first round determines all subsequent rounds

Cap table mistakes compound. A €350k angel round at a €2M valuation looks fine; the same angel round at a €700k valuation creates dilution that can prevent the venture from raising a Series A. Model the future cap table before signing today's term sheet.

Three concepts to bring to class

  1. Pre-money vs Post-money (F63) — Pre + Investment = Post. Your dilution depends on Post. Memorise it.
  2. Cap table (F62) — Be ready to update one in real time during tomorrow's hackathon.
  3. Funding curve (F61) — Understand which round you're at and what the next round needs to look like.

Connection to ILOs

Today's reading supports ILO 5 directly (Select the right funding for a business idea and pitch the business plan). The Constraint Cards Hackathon tests whether students can apply this thinking under pressure rather than just recite it.

Reading prompt

If your venture had to be fully funded by Q3 2026 and VC was completely unavailable, what would your funding stack look like? Be specific about sources, amounts, and timing.
