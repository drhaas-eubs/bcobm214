Why Big Firms Hire Entrepreneurs

Mature companies are good at running today's business and structurally bad at building tomorrow's. Why — and what to do about it. Read before Session 6. ~12 minutes.

The Innovator's Inheritance

A company that has succeeded for two decades has, by definition, optimised for its current business. Its decision-making, hiring, capital allocation, performance management and culture all reward delivering the existing value proposition more efficiently. This is exactly what produces the success.

It is also exactly what blocks the company from building anything materially different. Christensen called this the Innovator's Dilemma (F39): the practices that make incumbents successful in their current business systematically prevent them from responding to disruption. Pinchot identified the cultural dimension and prescribed a code of conduct for the people working against the grain inside such firms — the intrapreneurs (F41).

Three Frames for Reading the Problem

FrameDiagnosisPrescription
Three Horizons (F43)Most firms over-invest in H1, under-invest in H2 and H3Allocate explicitly across all three horizons; protect H3 from H1 metrics
3-Box Solution (F42)Most firms manage the present (Box 1) and avoid forgetting the past (Box 2); the future (Box 3) gets squeezedMake Box 2 forgetting work explicit and culturally sanctioned
Pinchot's 10 Commandments (F41)The corporate immune system rejects entrepreneurial people and projectsBuild cultural rituals that protect intrapreneurs; sponsor them at the senior level

Why hiring entrepreneurs matters

The most common corporate-innovation move of the past decade — building incubators, hosting hackathons, running design sprints — addresses the symptom (slow innovation) without addressing the cause (organisational antibodies). Hiring or retaining individual entrepreneurial people, then giving them the air cover to work against the grain, addresses the cause directly.

But it requires the senior team to recognise an intrapreneur when one walks in the room. The Helvetia case (in tomorrow's session) tests exactly that recognition.

Three concepts to bring to class

  1. Box 2 forgetting. What practice, assumption or product is your example firm protecting that it should be retiring?
  2. H3 starvation. Where is the H3 spend in your example firm? If it is below 10% of innovation budget, what is being lost in 10-year option value?
  3. Pinchot's commandments. Re-read the ten commandments. Which one would you find hardest to live by inside a large firm?

Connection to ILOs

Today's reading supports ILO 2 (Investigate the purpose of business development) and ILO 4 (Distinguish business development from other functions). Corporate intrapreneurship is one of the cleanest cases where BD must operate as a boundary-spanner between strategy, operations and culture — Sørensen's market-oriented BD (F46) made institutional.

Reading prompt

If your current employer (or a firm you know well) had to fund one H3 initiative this year, who in the firm would you trust to lead it — and what would have to change for the firm to actually fund it?
