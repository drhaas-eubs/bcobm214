The Lean Startup Compass

Why the smartest founders treat their idea as a hypothesis, not a promise. Read before Session 2. ~12 minutes.

The Core Idea

Eric Ries (2011) did something unusual for the entrepreneurship literature: he recast the founder not as a visionary but as an experimenter. The founder's primary job, in this view, is not to execute a plan but to test a chain of assumptions before the plan is locked in.

The mechanism is the Build-Measure-Learn loop:

  • Build the smallest possible version of your idea that lets a real customer encounter it.
  • Measure what they actually do — not what they say they would do.
  • Learn whether the assumption you tested holds, fails, or needs sharpening.

Each loop produces validated learning. The faster the loop turns, the cheaper learning becomes — and the cheaper learning is, the more honest founders can afford to be with themselves.

The Three Hardest Questions for Your Idea

Bring written answers to Session 2

  1. Who is the specific first customer — not a segment, a person you could call?
  2. What is the smallest test you could run in 7 days, for under €200, that would tell you whether they want it?
  3. What result of that test would convince you to kill the idea?

Many founders can answer the first question. Most can answer the second. Almost none come prepared with a sincere answer to the third — and that is precisely the answer that separates lean entrepreneurs from idea-romantics.

Connection to Unit 1

If Unit 1 was about why entrepreneurs start, Unit 2 is about how they avoid spending two years on the wrong thing. Effectuation gave you the logic of acting under uncertainty; the Lean Startup gives you the protocol for testing under uncertainty. They are siblings, not rivals.

Reading prompt

If you knew, with certainty, that your favourite idea would not work — what would be the very first sign? And how soon would it appear if you started building this Friday?
