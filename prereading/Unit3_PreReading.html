Why Founders Talk to Strangers

Discovery interviews are not market research. They are the only way to find out whether your customer is real. Read before Session 3. ~10 minutes.

The Founder's Most Expensive Mistake

The most expensive mistake a founder can make is not building the wrong product. It is building the wrong product well. The avoidable cause is almost always the same: discovery interviews that produced the validation the founder wanted, not the truth the founder needed.

Rob Fitzpatrick (2013) wrote The Mom Test after watching dozens of founders run interviews that were, in effect, just elaborate ways of asking their mothers if their idea was good — and then believing them.

The Three Mom Test Rules

RuleWhat it meansBad question · Good question
Talk about their life, not your ideaThe interview is not a pitch. Your idea contaminates honest answers."Would you use a focus-space app?" → "What did you do last week when you couldn't focus?"
Ask about specifics in the past, not generics in the futurePeople are bad predictors of their own behaviour and excellent reporters of their own past."How often would you use it?" → "When did you last pay for something to be more focused?"
Talk less, listen moreIf you're talking, you're not learning.Track talk-time ratio. Aim for <30% founder.

The Three Discovery Killers

If you find yourself doing any of these, stop

1. Compliment fishing. "What do you think?" / "Is this a good idea?" — answers you cannot bank on.
2. Future-tense hypotheticals. "Would you use…?" — almost always answered with the polite version of yes.
3. Pitching disguised as a question. "Imagine if we built X — wouldn't that be great?" — only useful for measuring the politeness of strangers.

The Three Discovery Signals That Mean Something

Treat as gold

1. "I bought…" — past financial commitment is a hard signal.
2. "It cost me…" — they remember the price; the problem matters.
3. "I tried these alternatives…" — the more they've tried, the more painful the problem.

What to Bring to Class

By Unit 3, every student must have spoken with at least two potential customers. Bring written notes — not summaries, but direct quotes. We will work them in the role play.

Connection to JTBD

Discovery interviews are how you find the actual job the customer is hiring solutions to do. Christensen's milkshake study found that morning-commute milkshake buyers were not hiring milkshakes for breakfast. They were hiring them to "make the long, boring drive less boring without making me too full." That insight was invisible until somebody listened to past behaviour, not stated preference.

Reading prompt

What is the question you have been afraid to ask your potential customer?
