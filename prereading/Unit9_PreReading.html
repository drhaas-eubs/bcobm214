Why Investors Stop Listening

Most pitches lose the room within 90 seconds — for predictable reasons. Read before Session 9. ~10 minutes.

The Attention Curve

Experienced investors decide whether they are interested in a pitch within the first 60-90 seconds. They do not consciously decide; they form a hypothesis. The remaining 18 minutes either confirm or weaken that hypothesis. If the first 90 seconds form a weak hypothesis, the remaining 18 minutes can rarely recover it.

The implication: every choice in the opening 90 seconds matters disproportionately. The frameworks in this unit (F65 10/20/30, F66 Sequoia, F67 SUCCESs, F68 Resonate) are all in service of those 90 seconds.

Five reasons investors stop listening

ReasonDiagnosisAntidote
1. Vague problemThe pitch describes a category instead of a specific customer painOpen with one specific customer's story (Heath SUCCESs · Concrete + Stories)
2. Founder overtureThe pitch starts with company history or founder credentialsOpen with the customer aha-moment, save credentials for slide 9 (Sequoia template, F66)
3. Inflated TAM'$50 billion market' with no bottom-up groundingUse TAM-SAM-SOM (F23) with bottom-up SOM defended explicitly
4. No 'why now'The pitch describes what the company does without explaining why this opportunity is uniquely available nowMake 'why now' an explicit slide; reference the regulatory, technological or behavioural shift that just opened the window
5. Missing economic logicThe pitch describes growth without explaining unit economicsShow how the business makes money on a per-unit basis, not just at aggregate

The opening 90 seconds — three patterns that work

Pattern A · The aha-moment

Walk the listener through the precise moment a customer first experiences the value. "Maria opens her phone, films her handwritten notes, and watches the text appear searchable and editable in her notes app. The seven-step workflow she did before is now one step." This pattern works for consumer products and any product with a visible customer experience.

Pattern B · The startling number

Open with a single number that makes the problem unignorable. "European pharmacies throw away €1.2 billion of unused medications each year." Best for pitches where the magnitude of the opportunity is the most compelling element.

Pattern C · The contrarian frame

Open with a widely-held belief, then explain why you think it's wrong. "Everyone says brick-and-mortar retail is dying. We think the most profitable retail businesses of the next decade will be physical-only — and here's why." Works when your strategic insight is the most compelling element.
