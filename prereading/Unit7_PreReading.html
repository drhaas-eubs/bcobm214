When the Founder Becomes the Bottleneck

Why the qualities that built the company are the same qualities that, at scale, prevent it from continuing to grow. Read before Session 7. ~12 minutes.

The Predictable Crisis

Larry Greiner observed in 1972 that growing organisations pass through five predictable phases — Creativity, Direction, Delegation, Coordination, Collaboration — and that each phase ends in a predictable crisis that forces structural transition to the next. The first crisis, between Phase 1 (Creativity) and Phase 2 (Direction), is the Leadership Crisis. The founder who built the company through pure creative drive must either evolve into a delegator, or step aside.

Most founders find this crisis the hardest moment of their venture life. The data shows them what their team and investors already know: the same intuition, hands-on involvement and personal customer relationships that built the company are now the constraint on its growth. The instinctive response — work harder — makes things worse.

Three Lenses on the Same Transition

FrameDiagnosis of the moment
Greiner's Curve (F49)Leadership Crisis: founder's personal style no longer scales; needs structural delegation
Adizes' Lifecycle (F50)Adolescence: founder must let go of operational decisions to allow the company to professionalise
Churchill & Lewis (F51)Take-off stage: the choice is between disengage-and-harvest or grow-and-reinvest, with very different leadership demands

What "evolution" actually requires

Founders who successfully navigate the transition typically do three things — none of them easy:

  1. Hire people whose work they could not do themselves. Most founder-CEOs hire smart people they could replace. The transition begins when they hire people who do things the founder cannot.
  2. Let early decisions be made worse than they would be made by the founder personally. Delegation isn't really delegation if every decision returns to the founder for approval. The team's first 30 days of "worse" decisions is the price of the team's eventual ability to make better ones.
  3. Replace founder-as-product with founder-as-system. The founder's intuition has to become the team's standards, processes and rituals. This is unglamorous but is the only way the company outlasts the founder's bandwidth.

When evolution doesn't happen

Three trajectories

Founders who don't navigate the transition usually follow one of three paths:
1. Burnout exit — the founder leaves involuntarily, often after a health event
2. Forced replacement — the board recruits a professional CEO, often with significant company turbulence
3. Plateau and decline — the founder remains; the company stops growing; talent leaves; competitors overtake

What the board can do

Boards are not bystanders to this transition. The most useful boards do four things:

  1. Recognise the crisis before the founder does
  2. Provide the founder with a real coach (not just a mentor) who has navigated the same transition
  3. Pre-discuss succession options openly, not after a crisis forces the conversation
  4. Protect the founder from the false binary "stay as CEO or leave" by mapping the spectrum of evolved-founder roles (Chief Product Officer, Founder & Chair, Founder Coach to Successor)

Connection to ILOs

Today's reading supports ILO 2 (Investigate the purpose of business development) and ILO 4 (Distinguish business development from other functions). Growth-stage BD work is fundamentally different from start-up-stage BD: it is about institutionalising patterns rather than discovering them.
