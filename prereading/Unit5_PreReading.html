Six Patterns Behind Failed Plans

Most startup failures follow recognisable patterns — and most of those patterns are visible in the original plan. Read before Session 5. ~12 minutes.

The Core Insight

Tom Eisenmann (Harvard Business School) spent more than a decade studying failed startups. The common story — "the founders pivoted but ran out of runway" — was usually accurate but unhelpful. Eisenmann sought the underlying patterns that made certain ventures predictably fragile from day one.

His finding: most failures cluster into six recurring patterns. Recognising your trajectory inside one of them lets you take corrective action before terminal failure — but only if you build the recognition into the plan itself.

The Six Patterns

PatternMechanismExample
Bad BedfellowsFounder-investor or co-founder misalignment surfaces under stressCo-founders disagreed on growth pace; investor pushed for revenue while founder wanted product depth
False StartBuilt and shipped without sufficient customer discovery; product solves the wrong problemSaaS startup discovered after launch that target buyer rarely uses competitor products either
False PromiseEarly traction looked like product-market fit; was actually one good channel or one good monthApp went viral on TikTok in week 6; never replicated; CAC quadrupled
Help WantedFailed to attract or retain critical talent in timeCouldn't hire CTO replacement after early CTO departure; technical debt compounded
Cascading MiraclesPlan requires multiple unproven things to all go right; each "miracle" reduces P(success)Plan needed regulatory approval AND new technology AND a partner deal AND celebrity endorsement
Speed TrapOver-invested in growth before unit economics worked; can't unwind without bankruptcySubscription startup raised €5M to scale acquisition before knowing whether retention was acceptable

How to read this list

Most ventures show traces of multiple patterns

The diagnosis is rarely "you have pattern X". The diagnosis is "you have a 4-on-Bad-Bedfellows, 4-on-False-Promise, and 5-on-Speed-Trap, and the cumulative weakness will kill you within 9 months." The point of the audit is to surface the cumulative risk early enough to redesign.

How to use the patterns in plan-writing

Each section of a Sahlman-frame plan (People · Opportunity · Context · Risk & Reward) maps to specific patterns:

  • People sections should pre-empt Bad Bedfellows and Help Wanted — by addressing co-founder alignment and the talent pipeline explicitly
  • Opportunity sections should pre-empt False Start and False Promise — by showing real customer evidence, not aggregate market data
  • Context sections should pre-empt Cascading Miracles — by listing every assumption the plan depends on
  • Risk & Reward sections should pre-empt Speed Trap — by showing the unit economics required for the plan to work

Plans that ignore the patterns get funded; plans that confront them get funded and survive.

Connection to ILOs

Today's reading supports ILO 1 (Develop a comprehensive business plan) and ILO 3 (Critically review the key aspect of entrepreneurship). The Eisenmann audit is one of the few teachable disciplines that genuinely separates surviving ventures from dying ones.

Reading prompt

Of Eisenmann's six patterns, which is your own venture concept most vulnerable to today — and what would the plan need to say to honestly address it?
